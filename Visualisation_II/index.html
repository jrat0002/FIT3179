Australian Domestic Air Travel

How has Australia's demand for domestic air travel changed over time?

A data visualisation by Joshua Ratten

It is clear that the Australian domestic air industry has grown significantly over the past 35+ years with a roughly 4 fold increase in the monthly number of flights over that time. Comparing different time periods on the chart above shows how the demand for specific routes has changed over time as more and more routes have been added.


Furthermore, the impact of significant global and Australian events is reflected in the chart above. Significant drops in air travel can be attributed to the 1989 Australian Pilots' Dispute, 2001 September 11 attacks, and Covid-19 pandemic.

Seasonal variations

Is there a yearly pattern to Australian air travel?

Clear seasonal variations can be seen in the way Australians utilise domestic air travel. Overall, when considering all air routes, the months of August and November show a clear yearly increase in the number of passengers flying domestically. Furthermore, the passenger load factor (number of used aircraft seats divided by total number of seats) shows similar yearly repeated trends.


The impact of Covid-19 on the air travel industry can be seen by a much lower passenger load factor in 2020.

Airport usage

Has airport usage changed over time?

Ranking airports by domestic travellers


The 3 busiest airports by domestic passengers have held a consistent rank for the entire duration of this dataset. These airports are Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane respectively.


The ranks of all other airports have not remained consistent. Darwin and Newcastle have seen increased popularity, whereas Alice Springs has dropped significantly in rank.

Domestic vs. International passengers


The overall proportion of domestic flights has remained steady over time at around 80%. However, the 3 busiest airports, Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane, have been steadily taking on an increasing proportion of international travellers over the past 10 years.


The Australian Pilots' Dispute of 1989 can be clearly seen as a sharp drop in the domestic proportion. Domestic pilots reducing their hours meant that international flights accounted for a higher proportion of flights.
